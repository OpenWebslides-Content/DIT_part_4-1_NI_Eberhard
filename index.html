Time dependent neutron imaging

  • intensity limitations: number of neutrons N per cm2 and second ~ 108
  • pixel size ~ 0.1 mm * 0.1 mm -> 104 pixels per cm2
  • intensity per pixel and second: 104 (if detection efficiency = 100%)
  • signal/noise-ratio ~ sqrt (N) – 100 under these conditions
  • reduction in exposure time down to 0.01s possible: S/N=1
  • further reduction by increasing the pixels (binning)
  • multiple exposure will increase the image quality

=> time dependent neutron imaging takes place in the range of milli-seconds to seconds

Continuous runs

  • several frames per second possible
  • read-out delay for CCD cameras
  • no delay for CMOS cameras
  • long-term scans over hours and days
  • referencing procedure when setup is fixed
  • correlation to other measurement parameters (mass-flow, temperature, humidity)

Quantification: Scattering Correction

In most elements (particularly hydrogen) the attenuation is due to neutron scattering instead of absorption.

The scattering contribution in the image means a blurring and makes a quantification of the image data difficult.

  • visible for Al, Fe, Cu, Ni (>0) clearly with a high-resolution detector

  • no high coherence demand
  • no mono-chromatic beam required
  • reflection and refraction seen as main reasons

Neutron Microscope: CONCEPT

  1. Optimized magnification lens (light throughput, resolution, spectral response, 1:5 magnification)
  2. Camera with high sensitivity and large pixel number > 4000
  3. Ultra-thin but efficient scintillator
  4. Highest possible beam intensity and collimation
  5. Focusing mirrored guide with 1 cm2 spot size

Exposure time per frame less than 10 minutes
